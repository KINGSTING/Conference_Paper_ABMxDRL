\begin{abstract}
Effective municipal solid waste (MSW) segregation is a critical sustainability bottleneck in developing urban centers[cite: 5]. This implementation gap is exemplified by the ``MRF Paradox''—a scenario where localized increases in waste infrastructure are countered by a concurrent surge in illegal dumping[cite: 6]. Standard Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) fails to resolve this via policy optimization due to the sparse reward problem caused by cultural behavioral inertia[cite: 7]. To address this, we introduce a socio-technical framework coupling an Agent-Based Model (ABM) with a Heuristic-Guided DRL (HuDRL) agent for predictive governance[cite: 8]. Using Bacolod, Lanao del Norte as a testbed, household cognition is modeled via the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)[cite: 9]. Global Sensitivity Analysis proves that internal household friction is the dominant driver of non-compliance, accounting for 79\% of system variance[cite: 10]. To navigate this non-convex policy space, our Markov Decision Process (MDP) utilizes action masking and heuristic guardrails[cite: 11]. Simulations reveal that while standard policies stagnate, HuDRL converges on a ``Sequential Saturation'' strategy—concentrating funding into single nodes to trigger localized social tipping points[cite: 12]. This approach establishes a rigorous mathematical framework for algorithmic governance and the data-driven optimization of limited public funds[cite: 13].
\end{abstract}

\section{INTRODUCTION}
In the Philippines, despite the strict mandates of R.A. 9003 [1], a systemic implementation gap known as the ``MRF Paradox'' persists: while mandated Material Recovery Facilities (MRFs) increased by 8.7\% between 2023 and 2024, illegal dumpsites concurrently surged by 84\% [14][cite: 16]. This indicates that infrastructure alone is insufficient to override deeply ingrained cultural non-compliance[cite: 17]. Current municipal trial-and-error resource allocation is no longer fiscally or legally viable; [cite: 18] Local Government Units (LGUs) require advanced predictive analytics to operationalize accountability and mathematically justify public expenditure[cite: 19]. Building a resilient society requires intelligent systems capable of optimizing these multi-agent environments characterized by bounded rationality and cultural inertia[cite: 33]. However, from a computational perspective, applying standard Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to such socio-economic systems introduces a severe manifestation of the ``Sparse Reward Problem'' [8][cite: 34]. Because human behavioral shifts require sustained, concentrated pressure before triggering a measurable state change, standard AI agents often fail to establish causal links between short-term budgetary actions and long-term environmental rewards[cite: 20].

To resolve this deficit, we propose a coupled Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) and DRL architecture[cite: 21]. By integrating the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) into the ABM, we simulate the structural friction and social hysteresis that define urban waste management[cite: 22]. Operating atop this environment, a Heuristic-Guided DRL (HuDRL) agent representing the central municipal authority dynamically optimizes budget allocations to systematically overcome behavioral inertia within strict fiscal constraints[cite: 23].

\section{RELATED WORK}
Designing cost-efficient policy requires psychological grounding. The TPB posits intention as a function of Attitude, Norms, and Control [7], yet environmental compliance is frequently overridden by the ``Cost of Effort'' [12][cite: 25]. We distinguish between the lower tipping point ($\sim$25-40\%) required to trigger initial adoption cascades [3], and the higher threshold at which conformity becomes a self-sustaining cultural lock-in [4][cite: 26]. For the purposes of this simulation, we implement the latter as the ``Social Norm Shield,'' a mathematically hard-locked state triggered when localized compliance reaches or exceeds 70\%[cite: 27]. Traditional optimization fails to capture such non-linear social hysteresis [2][cite: 28]. While existing research extensively targets MSW logistics [13], behavioral bottlenecks remain understudied[cite: 29]. By formalizing environments as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), neural networks can discover adaptive strategies, provided heuristics and action masking ensure convergence within rigid fiscal boundaries [8][cite: 30].

\section{SIMULATION ARCHITECTURE AND ENVIRONMENT DESIGN}
This study integrates ABM with DRL optimization within a strict polycentric governance framework[cite: 32]. The simulation is geographically bounded to Bacolod, Lanao del Norte, a 4th class municipality. It operates on a spatially explicit, non-toroidal $50\times50$ grid containing 4,022 Household Agents partitioned into seven distinct component barangays (Poblacion, Liangan East, Esperanza, Binuni, Demologan, Mati, and Babalaya)[cite: 34]. 

The architecture utilizes a dual-clock temporal resolution to bridge micro-behavior and macro-policy[cite: 35]. Household agents execute their decision-making logic on daily micro-ticks, simulating the continuous generation and management of waste[cite: 38]. Conversely, the central Mayor Agent evaluates the environment and executes budget distributions on quarterly (90-day) epochs over a 1,080-tick (3-year) simulation horizon[cite: 39]. 

The core architectural novelty is the continuous feedback loop between the discrete ABM and the continuous MDP[cite: 40]. To prevent the neural network from being overwhelmed by the micro-state data of 4,022 households, the architecture utilizes meso-level Barangay Agents as data aggregators[cite: 41]. These agents compress the daily micro-behavioral outputs into a unified 17-dimensional state vector in $\mathcal{O}(N)$ time, providing a clear observation space for the Mayor Agent's MDP[cite: 42]. The complete polycentric simulation architecture is illustrated in Figure 1[cite: 43]. Macro-policy and budget allocations execute on quarterly epochs, while micro-behavior and enforcement resolve on daily ticks[cite: 57, 58].

\section{THE MULTI-AGENT ENVIRONMENT}

\subsection{Household Agents and the Theory of Planned Behavior}
Household agents operate as continuous finite state machines. To ensure population heterogeneity, behavioral weights are instantiated via stochastic Gaussian sampling $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^{2})$, bounded within [0.1,0.6][cite: 61]. Their cognition is governed by a dynamically weighted TPB utility equation $(U_{seg})$[cite: 62]. We contribute a novel extension to the foundational work of Ceschi et al. by introducing a temporal decay factor (t) to the cognitive weights and formalizing the structural friction[cite: 63, 64]. The utility is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
U_{seg}=(w_{A}(t)A)+(w_{SN}(t)SN_{loc})+(w_{PBC}(t)PBC)-C_{Net}+\epsilon \label{eq:1}
\end{equation}
[cite: 65, 66, 67]

where A, $SN_{loc},$ and PBC represent Attitude, localized Subjective Norms, and Perceived Behavioral Control[cite: 68]. To ensure realistic heterogeneity, decision-making is buffered by stochastic human noise $(\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,0.05)$ bounded at [-0.1, 0.1]) and subject to a natural decay rate ($O_{dec}=0.01$ per tick) over time[cite: 69].

To allow the DRL agent to interact with psychological barriers, we mathematically formalized the Net Cost $(C_{Net})$ function:

\begin{equation}
C_{Net}=C_{Effort}+\eta(C_{Mon}-I-F\cdot P_{Detect}) \label{eq:2}
\end{equation}
[cite: 70, 71]

Crucially, $C_{Effort}$ is strictly defined not as external travel distance, but as the internal household friction of washing, sorting, and storing segregated waste[cite: 72]. This physical hassle is weighed against socioeconomic financial variables: tangible costs $(C_{Mon})$, municipal incentives (I), and expected fines $(F\times P_{Detect})$ [6][cite: 73]. These financial vectors are scaled by $\eta$, an income-sensitivity multiplier[cite: 74].

To model cultural inertia, agents query their spatial environment using an $\mathcal{O}(k)$ Moore neighborhood algorithm[cite: 75]. The simulation incorporates a ``Bayanihan'' transitional state, establishing a 40\% compliance floor[cite: 76]. This acts as a mathematical minimum baseline $(SN_{loc}\leftarrow \max(SN_{loc},0.40))$, representing a psychological floor where motivation is sustained by fundamental community reciprocity rather than defaulting to purely enforcement-driven behavior. If local compliance breaches the critical 70\% tipping point $(SN_{loc}\ge0.70)$ the behavior internalizes, mathematically hard-locking the subjective norm weight $(w_{sn}(t)=0.90)$[cite: 77]. 

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{The Household's Daily Decision Process [cite: 78]}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} A, $SN_{loc}$, PBC, and $C_{Effort}$
\STATE \textbf{Step 1: Assess Social Norms} $(SN_{loc})$
\STATE Scan k-Moore neighborhood for raw compliance $(SN_{raw})$
\STATE Apply Bayanihan floor: $SN_{loc}\leftarrow \max(SN_{raw},0.40)$
\IF{$SN_{loc}\ge70\%$}
\STATE Internalize habit: Set $w_{sn}=0.90$ (Habit Shield)
\ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{Step 2: Calculate Utility} $(U_{seg})$ (Eq. 1)
\STATE \textbf{Step 3: Weigh Factors}
\STATE Weigh A, $SN_{loc}$, and PBC against $C_{Net}$ (Eq. 2)
\STATE \textbf{Step 4: Execute Action}
\IF{$U_{seg}>0$}
\STATE Action: Segregate waste (Compliance)
\ELSE
\STATE Action: Dispose of mixed waste (Non-compliance)
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Enforcement and Barangay Agents}
Enforcement agents physicalize the $P_{Detect}$ risk profile. Operating under bounded rationality, they execute stochastic random walks and localized spatial queries rather than possessing global oversight[cite: 80]. The model differentiates between Micro-Enforcement (local Tanods with a 90\% distraction rate) and Macro-Enforcement (LGU Strike Force with a cost of PHP 12,000 per enforcer and PHP 1,000 per citation)[cite: 81]. Their deployments are bound by strict 30-day temporal contracts to simulate real-world municipal payroll realities [11][cite: 82]. 

Barangay Agents serve as meso-level administrative nodes executing a polycentric Cascading Incentive Distribution Protocol[cite: 83]. When households claim rewards, the agent draws first from its static Internal Revenue Allotment (IRA) and second from the Mayor's dynamic augmentation budget[cite: 84]. If both are exhausted, the household suffers a computational ``Perceived Unfairness'' penalty, severely degrading intrinsic motivation [12][cite: 85].

\section{THE DEEP REINFORCEMENT LEARNING AGENT}

\subsection{MDP Formulation}
The central policymaker is formalized as an episodic MDP defined by the tuple (S, A, P, R, $\gamma$)[cite: 92]:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State Space (S):} A 17-dimensional continuous vector mathematically representing the municipal ecosystem[cite: 93]. It is composed of: 7 dimensions for the aggregated quarterly compliance rates of the respective barangays, 7 dimensions for localized budget exhaustion rates, 1 dimension for global Political Capital, 1 dimension for the global municipal budget remaining, and 1 dimension for the quarter index (time step)[cite: 94].
    \item \textbf{Action Space (A):} A 21-dimensional continuous vector $(A_{t}\in[-1,1]^{21})$. This represents the network outputs corresponding to the quarterly budget across 3 distinct fiscal instruments (Information Campaigns, Enforcement, and Incentives) for each of the 7 locales $(7\times3=21)$[cite: 95]. The Actor outputs the mean of a Gaussian policy with learned log-standard deviation; raw actions are sampled from this distribution, clipped to [-1,1], and subsequently mapped to the probability simplex $\Delta^{20}$ via a modified Softmax function to guarantee the allocations sum to exactly 100\% of available funds[cite: 96, 97]. Because a standard Softmax over the [-1,1] bounds limits the maximum weight of any single component to approximately 7\%, our implementation utilizes $\tau=5.0$ as a multiplicative gain factor (inverse temperature)[cite: 98]. This dynamically scales the raw network logits prior to normalization, mathematically sharpening the distribution to enable the extreme 69\% resource concentration observed in the agent's converged ``Sequential Saturation'' strategy[cite: 99].
\end{itemize}

The central constraint is an annual municipal budget of PHP 1,500,000, discretized to a maximum quarterly operating budget $(B_{q})$ of PHP 375,000[cite: 101].

The environment was built utilizing the OpenAI Gymnasium API and trained using the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) implementation from Stable-Baselines3[cite: 103]. To stabilize gradient updates across the 21-dimensional continuous action space, the training utilized a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MlpPolicy) and parallelized data collection across a SubprocVecEnv with 4 CPU cores[cite: 104]. \subsection{Multi-Objective Reward Shaping and Heuristics}
To solve the sparse reward problem inherent in directing human cultural shifts, the MDP utilizes a composite reward function $(R_{t})$ designed to provide dense feedback signals throughout the simulation horizon[cite: 100]:

\begin{equation}
R_{t}=\omega_{1}R_{Base}+\omega_{2}R_{Jackpot}+\omega_{3}R_{Focus}-\omega_{4}P_{Waste}+\Phi_{cap} \label{eq:3}
\end{equation}
[cite: 106]

The term $R_{Base}$ provides a linear reward proportional to the global compliance rate across all 4,022 households[cite: 107]. To incentivize the breach of cultural inertia, $R_{Jackpot}$ functions as a discrete step-reward, granting a +200 point bonus whenever a specific barangay crosses the 70\% compliance threshold for the first time[cite: 108]. The $R_{Focus}$ term encourages the discovered ``Sequential Saturation'' strategy by rewarding localized compliance improvements in the single weakest barangay, while $P_{Waste}$ applies a quadratic penalty for allocating any portion of the municipal budget to barangays that have already ``graduated'' past the 70\% threshold[cite: 109]. Finally, $\Phi_{cap}$ serves as the Political Capital Modifier. It is defined mathematically as $\Phi_{cap}=100\cdot \ln(PC/0.25)$ when Political Capital $(PC)$ is between 0.10 and 0.25, dropping to 0 for $PC>0.25$[cite: 110]. If $PC<0.10$, a terminal penalty of -1000 is applied and the episode immediately truncates[cite: 111].

To ensure training stability, the agent is constrained by heuristic guardrails (Algorithm 2)[cite: 112]. Most notably, the ``Phase-Shift Crisis Guardrail'' acts as an operational failsafe: if global municipal compliance falls below the 40\% Bayanihan floor, the MDP mathematically forces at least 50\% of the budget to the weakest locale to prevent systemic behavioral failure[cite: 113]. This weighted structure ensures that the agent prioritizes both environmental outcomes and fiscal sustainability without incurring a political collapse[cite: 114].

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{The Mayor Agent's Triage \& Safety Heuristics [cite: 115]}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Current compliance levels and AI's proposed budget
\STATE \textbf{Rule 1: The Graduation Mechanism}
\FOR{each barangay}
\IF{barangay compliance $>$ 70\%}
\STATE Slash its proposed allocation by 99\% (retaining 1\% to prevent policy gradient collapse)
\ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Rule 2: The Phase-Shift Crisis Guardrail}
\IF{global municipal compliance $<$ 40\%}
\STATE Identify the weakest performing barangay
\STATE Guarantee it receives at least half of the total budget
\ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{Rule 3: Strict Fiscal Normalization}
\STATE Proportionally renormalize all allocations (dividing by the new sum) so they total exactly 100\%, inherently redistributing any funds freed by Rule 1.
\STATE \textbf{return} Final executed budget distribution
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{RESULTS AND DISCUSSION}

\subsection{Calibration and Global Sensitivity Analysis}
Empirical calibration anchored the model utilizing Municipal Environment and Natural Resources Office (MENRO) audits, establishing a valid Status Quo baseline of 12.5\% global compliance (counteracting inflated self-reports of over 50\%) [5][cite: 118]. A Sobol Global Sensitivity Analysis (GSA), summarized in Table II, revealed that the internal ``Cost of Effort'' $(C_{Effort})$ is the overwhelmingly dominant driver of behavior [9][cite: 119]. Yielding a Total-Order index $(S_{T})$ of 1.06 and accounting for 79\% of the variance in global compliance $(S_{1}\approx0.79)$, this mathematically validates the Intention-Action gap: if the internal friction of washing and sorting is too high, households default to non-compliance regardless of environmental beliefs[cite: 120]. This finding is consistent with recent Philippine barangay-level case studies highlighting structural friction and logistical realities as primary barriers to waste segregation [11][cite: 121]. Conversely, intrinsic Attitude weight $(w_{A})$ exhibited near-zero sensitivity, proving that Information Campaigns (IEC) suffer strict diminishing returns without localized structural support[cite: 122].

\subsection{Computational Convergence and Algorithm Comparison}
To ensure algorithmic robustness, all metrics were evaluated across independent random seeds $(n=5)$[cite: 146]. As shown in Table III, HuDRL achieved a mean reward of 10554.99 with extremely low variance $(\sigma=4.02)$. A Welch's t-test comparing HuDRL and the Greedy baseline yielded $p<0.001$[cite: 147]. The exceptionally large Cohen's d effect size of 139.11 is mathematically valid but is inflated by the near-zero standard deviation $(\sigma=1.2)$ of the Greedy baseline, which is essentially deterministic up to ABM stochasticity[cite: 148]. 

The practically relevant comparison is therefore between HuDRL and the Dirichlet baseline[cite: 128]. While HuDRL achieves a higher mean reward than Dirichlet sampling, the difference is not statistically significant at $n=5$ $(p=0.125)$, though the large effect size $(d=1.34)$ suggests significance would emerge with additional seeds[cite: 129]. HuDRL's principal advantage over random allocation is the substantially lower variance $(\sigma=4.0 \text{ vs. } \sigma=354.6)$ and the interpretable, transferable policy structure (``Sequential Saturation'') that a black-box random allocator cannot provide[cite: 130].

The HuDRL architecture exhibits stable convergence, whereas the Vanilla PPO agent remains trapped in the sparse-reward ``Collapse Zone''[cite: 131]. We operationally define this zone as a state space trap where the unguided agent cannot reliably trigger the 70\% tipping point step-rewards and subsequently suffers gradient collapse, stagnating at a mean reward of 1859.43[cite: 132]. Although foundational Deep Reinforcement Learning has achieved human-level control in games and robotics [15], Vanilla PPO failed to escape this zone in the current environment[cite: 133]. This failure highlights the necessity of the heuristic-guided triage and action masking implemented in the HuDRL architecture to overcome the unguided exploration of the sparse reward landscape [8][cite: 134].

\subsection{Comparative Analysis of Policy Strategies}
Simulation experiments revealed a stark divergence between traditional heuristics and the HuDRL approach[cite: 159]. The Status Quo policy (PHP 53,571.43 per locale) acted as a mathematical dilution mechanism, resulting in an ``Urban Resource Trap'' where city-wide compliance stagnated at 30.18\%[cite: 160]. Similarly, the Pure Enforcement strategy triggered a ``Draconian Trap,'' causing a terminal ``Political Collapse'' by Quarter 2 as public approval fell below the 10\% threshold[cite: 161]. 

The HuDRL algorithm autonomously executed a ``Sequential Saturation'' strategy, concentrating up to 69\% of available funds onto specific urban bottlenecks[cite: 162]. This targeted approach mirrors the logistical efficiency required in decentralized Philippine governance structures, where concentrating resources often yields more sustainable outcomes than uniform distribution [10][cite: 163]. Once a locale crossed the 70\% threshold, the AI exploited the ``Social Norm Shield'' to maintain compliance with minimal funding, successfully reaching 90.45\% sustained global compliance[cite: 164]. This systematic transition to Algorithmic Resource Targeting—dynamically shifting the entire fiscal weight to the next un-saturated bottleneck the moment the current locale achieves its habit shield—provides the necessary predictive layer for municipalities to optimize limited public funds[cite: 165].

\subsection{Limitations}
While providing a rigorous predictive framework, model validity is bounded by its calibration to a single 4th-class municipality, necessitating re-validation for disparate urban topologies[cite: 179]. Methodologically, although introducing temporal decay and formalizing $C_{Net}$, initial psychological parameters were literature-seeded rather than derived from local psychometric surveys[cite: 180]. Furthermore, deterministic penalization may underestimate inconsistent political will, and agents lack demographic heterogeneity beyond the income-sensitivity multiplier[cite: 181]. Finally, the 12-quarter horizon, while sufficient for initial phase-shifts, may not capture the multi-generational permanence of cultural internalization[cite: 182].

\section{CONCLUSION}
This study validates that coupling ABM with HuDRL provides a mathematically rigorous laboratory for policy optimization[cite: 184]. By formalizing municipal dynamics into an MDP, the framework demonstrates that standard DRL fails in socio-technical systems due to sparse rewards and cultural hysteresis [8][cite: 185]. The HuDRL architecture proves that overcoming household segregation friction requires targeted, sequential resource saturation, which is superior to broad, diluted mandates[cite: 186]. Deploying this framework allows LGUs to transition from reactive budgeting to precision-targeted mathematical strategies[cite: 187]. Future research will explore transitioning this architecture into a Municipal Digital Twin by integrating Vehicle Routing Problems (VRP) and Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) for commercial actors[cite: 188].

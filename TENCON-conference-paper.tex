\begin{abstract}
Effective municipal solid waste (MSW) segregation is a critical sustainability bottleneck. Applying Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to optimize policy in multi-agent human environments faces the sparse reward problem due to cultural inertia. We introduce a socio-technical framework coupling an Agent-Based Model (ABM) with a Heuristic-Guided DRL (HuDRL) agent for predictive governance. Using Bacolod, Lanao del Norte as a testbed, we optimize resource allocation via a Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) cognitive model. Global Sensitivity Analysis proves that internal household friction accounts for 79\% of system variance. To navigate this non-convex space, the HuDRL agent utilizes action masking and a targeted amplification algorithm. Simulations reveal that while standard policies stagnate, the HuDRL agent converges on a ``Sequential Saturation'' strategy—concentrating funding into single nodes until tipping points are breached. This approach proves vastly superior to equitable distribution, directly advancing Sustainable Development Goals 11, 12, and 16.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Sustainable society requires systems capable of optimizing complex human environments. Socio-economic systems are characterized by bounded rationality and cultural inertia, creating non-convex policy spaces where equitable resource distribution—a common human bias—is often suboptimal. From a computational view, standard DRL faces a severe ``Sparse Reward Problem''~\cite{zheng2022b} because human behavior shifts require sustained pressure before state changes occur.

This study uses Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) management as a testbed. In the Philippines, despite the mandates of R.A. 9003, Local Government Units (LGUs) struggle with the ``MRF Paradox'': while recovery facilities increased by 8.7\% in 2024, illegal dumpsites concurrently surged by 84\%~\cite{psa2025}. We propose a coupled ABM and DRL architecture integrating TPB to simulate structural friction. A HuDRL agent representing municipal authority then optimizes budget allocations to overcome behavioral inertia within strict fiscal realities.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Behavioral Friction and Tipping Points}
TPB posits that intention is a function of Attitude, Subjective Norms, and Perceived Behavioral Control~\cite{taraghi2025}. However, environmental compliance is frequently obstructed by the ``Cost of Effort''~\cite{moeini2023}. Sustained change relies on tipping points; once a minority reaches $\sim$25\%, it can trigger cascades across the majority~\cite{centola2018}, creating a ``Social Norm Shield'' where behavior resists decay at compliance $\ge$70\%~\cite{andre2021}.

\subsection{Algorithmic Policy Design}
Traditional optimization fails to capture non-linear social hysteresis~\cite{tian2024}. While research exists for MSW logistics~\cite{zhang2021}, behavioral bottlenecks remain understudied. ABMs offer virtual laboratories, but manual testing is labor-intensive. By formalizing environments as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), neural networks can discover adaptive strategies, provided they utilize heuristics and action masking to respect fiscal boundaries~\cite{zheng2022b}.

\section{System Architecture}
The simulation is bounded to Bacolod, Lanao del Norte, containing 4,022 Household Agents across seven barangays. The architecture uses a dual-clock temporal resolution: households act daily (micro-ticks), while the Mayor Agent optimizes budgets quarterly (90-day epochs) over a 3-year horizon. Meso-level Barangay Agents aggregate micro-data into a 17-dimensional state vector, reducing computational complexity to $\mathcal{O}(N)$.

\section{The Multi-Agent Environment}
\subsection{Household Agents and Cognitive Weights}
Household utility ($U_{seg}$) is governed by a weighted TPB equation:
\begin{equation}
U_{seg} = (w_{A}(t)A) + (w_{SN}(t)SN_{loc}) + (w_{PBC}(t)PBC) - C_{Net} + \epsilon
\end{equation}
where $C_{Net}$ represents internal household friction (sorting and storing) weighed against incentives and fines~\cite{zhao2022}. If local compliance breaches 70\%, behavior internalizes, hard-locking $w_{sn}(t)$ at 0.90.

\subsection{Enforcement and Local Administrative Nodes}
Enforcement agents execute stochastic random walks with a cost of PHP 12,000 per macro-enforcer. Barangay Agents execute a Cascading Incentive Protocol, drawing from local allotments and municipal augmentation. Exhausted budgets trigger a ``Perceived Unfairness'' penalty, degrading household motivation~\cite{moeini2023}.

\section{The Deep Reinforcement Learning Agent}
\subsection{MDP Formulation and Training}

\subsection{Reward Shaping}
The composite reward $R_t$ uses a +200 point $R_{Jackpot}$ for breaching the 70\% threshold and a $P_{Waste}$ quadratic penalty for redundant funding in ``graduated'' barangays. A logarithmic $\Phi_{cap}$ penalty prevents political collapse if approval drops below 25\%.

\subsection{Comparative Analysis}
HuDRL outperformed Vanilla PPO (Reward: 10551.7 vs 1728.6) by discovering a ``Sequential Saturation'' strategy. As shown in Fig 1, the AI concentrated $\ge$60\% of funds onto single barangays to force social tipping points. Once compliance reached 70\%, the ``Social Norm Shield'' maintained the state, allowing the agent to pivot funds to the next bottleneck. This method reached 90.45\% compliance, whereas equitable distribution stagnated at 30.18\%.

\subsection{Limitations}
Validity is bounded by the calibration to a single 4th-class municipality. Baseline psychological parameters were literature-seeded, and household agents lack deep demographic heterogeneity beyond income-sensitivity. Future work should explore MARL for commercial actors and 10-year horizons to test habit permanence.

\section{Conclusion}
HuDRL proves that building resilient societies requires abandoning equitable resource dilution in favor of targeted saturation. By formalizing policy as an MDP, LGUs can replace trial-and-error budgeting with precision-targeted mathematical strategies.
